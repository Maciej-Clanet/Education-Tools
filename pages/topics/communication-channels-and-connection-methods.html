Overview

What you should know by the end of this lesson

Data transmission questions often expect you to do more than name a cable or network. You usually need to explain how the channel behaves, what the connection method allows, and why that choice fits the scenario better than another option.

Learning goals

  • Define simplex, half-duplex, and full-duplex clearly.
  • Explain point-to-point and multi-drop links.
  • Compare common wired and wireless connection methods.
  • Justify a suitable connection choice for a real task.

Quick memory hooks

  • Simplex: one direction only.
  • Half-duplex: both ways, not at once.
  • Full-duplex: both ways at once.
  • Multi-drop: several devices share one link.

Where this matters

These ideas appear in printer links, sensor networks, office Ethernet, fibre backbones, Wi-Fi devices, Bluetooth peripherals, and industrial control systems.

Channel Types

Communication channels describe how data moves

A communication channel is the path or behaviour used for data transfer. Some channel types describe direction, while others describe how devices are arranged on the link.

Simplex one-way only Half-duplex turns are shared Full-duplex both directions together
Simplex Data travels in one direction only, such as a keyboard signal sent to a device or a one-way broadcast.
Half-duplex Both devices can transmit, but only one may send at a time, like a walkie-talkie conversation.
Full-duplex Both directions work at the same time, so sending and receiving can happen together.
Point-to-point One dedicated link joins exactly two devices, which can make control and fault isolation simpler.
Multi-drop Several devices share the same connection path, so access has to be managed carefully.

Worked comparison

Type What it means Typical example Key implication
Simplex Only one device sends on the channel. Broadcast radio or a basic sensor output No return path for acknowledgement.
Half-duplex Both ends send, but only one at a time. Walkie-talkies Sharing the line reduces clashes but slows dialogue.
Full-duplex Both ends send and receive simultaneously. Phone calls or switched Ethernet Faster interaction and fewer turn-taking delays.
Point-to-point Exactly two devices use the link. PC connected directly to a router port Performance is more predictable.
Multi-drop Many devices share one path. Several sensors on one bus Traffic control is needed to avoid conflicts.

Connection Methods

Different links suit different distances, speeds, and settings

A connection method is the practical way devices are joined. In a Unit 2 answer, it is usually stronger to explain the benefit of a method than to just name it.

Ethernet reliable wired LAN Fibre fast and long-distance Wi-Fi mobile and flexible Bluetooth short-range pairing

Methods you should recognise

Method Best for Strength Trade-off
Ethernet copper cable Offices, classrooms, fixed desktops Stable and usually lower latency Less mobility than wireless
Fibre optic Backbone links and long runs Very high bandwidth and low interference Higher installation cost
Wi-Fi Laptops, tablets, shared spaces No physical cable needed Signal strength and interference can vary
Bluetooth Peripherals and short-range pairing Convenient and low power Short range and lower throughput
Cellular mobile data Remote access away from local networks Wide coverage Data cost and variable signal quality

Why connection choice changes by scenario

A desktop in a computer room often suits Ethernet because the device stays in one place and benefits from a steady link. A warehouse tablet may need Wi-Fi because staff move around. A building-to-building backbone may justify fibre because it must carry a large amount of traffic over a longer distance.

Selection

Choose the link by matching the method to the task

Strong exam answers usually compare what the job needs against what the connection method can deliver.

Speed and bandwidth Video streams and large backups need a stronger link than a low-rate sensor reading.
Distance Some methods work only over short ranges, while others are better for long cable runs or whole-site coverage.
Mobility Wireless is often chosen when devices move around the space.
Interference and reliability Critical systems may need a more stable link with less susceptibility to disruption.
Security Open wireless access may need extra protection compared with a physically controlled cable path.
Cost and scale The best technical choice still has to be practical for the organisation to install and maintain.

Model answer shape

A stronger response sounds like this: "Wi-Fi is suitable because staff need to move between rooms, while the transmitted data volume is moderate and the network can cover the whole floor without new cable installation."

That is better than simply saying "Use Wi-Fi because it is wireless."

Exam Traps

Common mistakes with communication channels and links

These questions often reward precise terminology, so small slips can weaken an otherwise good answer.

Mixing up half-duplex and full-duplex

Students sometimes say both types allow two-way communication, but forget the timing difference.

  • Half-duplex means two-way, but not simultaneously.
  • Full-duplex means both directions at the same time.

Fix: Use the phrase "at the same time" when describing full-duplex.

Confusing point-to-point with one-way transmission

Point-to-point tells you how many devices share the link, not whether the data only goes one way.

  • Point-to-point can still be full-duplex.
  • Simplex is the one-way term.

Fix: Separate device arrangement from data direction.

Naming a method without justifying it

A bare label such as "use fibre" usually does not earn as much credit as a reasoned choice.

  • Explain the task need first.
  • Then link that need to the method's benefit.

Fix: Include a because-clause in your answer.

4 marks Explain

Question 1

Explain why a full-duplex point-to-point link would be more suitable than a half-duplex shared link for a manager taking live video calls from a fixed office PC.

Show answer guide

A strong answer compares the behaviour of both link types.

  • Full-duplex allows sending and receiving at the same time.
  • That suits live conversation because audio and video move in both directions together.
  • Point-to-point gives a dedicated path between the two communication devices.
  • A half-duplex shared link would create turn-taking or competition for the channel, which would reduce call quality.
5 marks Explain and justify

Question 2

A college wants to connect handheld barcode scanners used by staff walking around a warehouse. Explain which connection method would be suitable and justify your choice against at least two alternatives.

Show answer guide

The best answers match the device behaviour to the chosen link.

  • Wi-Fi is usually suitable because staff need mobility.
  • It allows scanners to stay connected while moving around the warehouse.
  • Ethernet would be more reliable but is impractical for a handheld moving device.
  • Bluetooth may be too short-range for a large warehouse.
  • A strong answer may also mention coverage, interference, security, and installation cost.
